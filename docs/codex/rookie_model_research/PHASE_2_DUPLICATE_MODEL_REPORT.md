# Deterministic Rookie Evaluation Model for the LVE Dynasty League

## Executive summary

For a deterministic rookie model tailored to this league, the research supports four different position models rather than one unified “rookie score.” Across public prospect models, peer-reviewed work, and public draft/combine studies, the most stable rule is that **actual NFL draft capital is the strongest single public input**, but it is not a pure talent measure. It bundles private scouting, medicals, interviews, scheme fit, and then changes future opportunity because teams are incentivized to justify the pick. That makes it essential, but also a source of circularity and bias. It must be the anchor, not the whole model. citeturn26view0turn40view0turn30view0turn35view1

For this exact league, the highest strategic conclusion is that **RB and WR should dominate rookie capital allocation**, but for different reasons. WR benefits from the starting format of three WR plus two flex and deep dynasty benches, so age-adjusted production and target-earning metrics should drive long-term value. RB benefits from the scoring rules: no PPR removes some pure-catch inflation, while the 0.4 rushing/receiving first-down bonus and 4-point rushing/receiving TDs push value toward players who earn real touches, stay on the field, move chains, and get goal-line work. citeturn24view1turn27view0turn31view0

QB should be modeled carefully but **de-prioritized in the final cross-positional board** because this is 1QB. The research is unusually clear that passing quality gets quarterbacks drafted, but rushing ability, scramble tendency, and pressure/sack management help explain who actually separates in the NFL and in fantasy. That matters, but scarcity is lower here than in Superflex, so even a good QB model should feed a smaller final position multiplier. citeturn30view0turn16view0turn42view0turn33view0

TE should be the most selective position. The public evidence for tight ends points to a familiar pattern: draft capital and receiving production/efficiency matter more than size-and-combine hype alone, though athleticism is more useful at TE than it is at WR. In a no-TE-premium league, most rookie TEs should lose to strong released veterans unless they check all three boxes at once: premium capital, receiving profile, and a credible route path. citeturn35view1turn14view0turn14view2turn37view0

The other league-specific conclusion is structural rather than statistical: **your draft room is not rookie-only**. Because at least one top-five officially ranked player must be released and the draft happens after roster declaration, the rookie model should produce only a **rookie prospect score**, then pass every rookie through a veteran comparison layer. In LVE, that veteran comparison should be most punitive against marginal QBs and non-elite TEs, and least punitive against early-career WRs with strong age-adjusted production.

## Assumptions and modeling principles

I assume the fantasy rookie draft occurs after the real entity["sports_league","NFL","us football league"] draft, so actual NFL pick number and landing spot are known. I also assume a local-first implementation can store historical cohorts and percentile maps, and can ingest public or licensed data from sources such as entity["company","PFF","football analytics"], entity["company","Fantasy Life","fantasy football media"], entity["company","ESPN","sports media"], NFL.com, and box-score sources. Public prospect models using that kind of data generally use modern samples because richer charting data is only broadly available from the late 2010s onward; where advanced charting is unavailable, longer-history box-score proxies can be substituted, but they should be mapped to the same feature roles rather than changing the architecture. citeturn26view0turn27view0turn24view1turn14view0turn16view0

The evidence in this report is separated by strength. **High** means peer-reviewed evidence or multiple public models converge on the same finding. **Medium** means repeated public model support and strong face validity, but limited peer-reviewed confirmation. **Low** means the signal is thin, unstable, or highly context-dependent. **Speculative** means no strong public validation was located and the feature should be treated as heuristic only.

The most important modeling rule is how to treat draft capital. It is valuable because it captures information the public model does not have, and public models repeatedly find it to be the strongest single variable. But it also misleads if treated as pure ability: teams can undervalue traits that later matter, and some positions have historically been drafted with criteria that do not line up perfectly with long-term production. The clearest public examples from this source set are quarterback rushing and tight end college receiving production. citeturn26view0turn40view0turn30view0turn35view1turn31view0

A second rule is **do not score raw college volume without context**. College production should be normalized for age, role, teammates, schedule strength, and offensive environment where possible. Public prospect models explicitly normalize production this way because team volume, target competition, and strength of schedule distort raw totals. citeturn24view1turn26view0turn14view0

A third rule is **athletic testing is mostly a secondary layer**, not the core. Public combine research finds that combine outcomes can relate more strongly to draft/matriculation than to long-term success, with mixed results across positions. The notable exceptions in this source set are RB speed score and TE athleticism, both of which show more usable signal than generic WR combine hype. citeturn17view0turn17view2turn27view0turn35view1turn37view0

A fourth rule is **separate prospect quality from rookie-year environment**. Landing spot, depth chart, line quality, and offensive environment can matter for Year 1 output and short-term trade value, but they are less stable than prospect-quality features for long-term dynasty ranking. In this league, that distinction matters because deep benches and 23 keepers make patience more available than in shallow redraft formats. citeturn14view0turn16view0turn24view1

```mermaid
flowchart LR
    A[College and pre-draft data] --> B[Normalize for age, role, teammates, schedule, and team style]
    B --> C[Position-specific prospect base score]
    C --> D[Confidence and risk layer]
    D --> E[Post-draft opportunity overlay]
    E --> F[LVE scoring overlay]
    F --> G[Compare every rookie to released veterans and free agents]
    G --> H[Offline draft board]
```

A practical implementation starting point is **70% prospect base, 20% post-draft opportunity overlay, and 10% LVE scoring overlay**, followed by a veteran comparison module. That **70/20/10 split is a design recommendation, not a research finding**.

## Quarterbacks

Quarterback research is the clearest example of why a league-specific model should distinguish **real-NFL success**, **fantasy upside**, and **1QB league economics**. Public and peer-reviewed work agree that passing ability gets quarterbacks selected, but among drafted quarterbacks, rushing adds critical separating power. Public QB prospect models also highlight scramble rate, pressure-to-sack rate, and other pressure-management measures as useful ways to separate fantasy-relevant profiles from merely decent passers. In LVE, that matters, but the 1QB format means the final position multiplier should still be conservative. citeturn30view0turn16view0turn42view0turn33view0

Do **not** overweight raw passing TD totals, clean-pocket box scores, or raw completion percentage. Public work shows accuracy refined for drops and non-aimed throws is better than raw completion percentage, and peer-reviewed QB work suggests traditional passing measures among drafted passers do less to separate NFL hits than rushing and more robust composite measures. citeturn33view0turn30view0

The main failure mode is the pocket passer with good draft capital and clean, efficient college passing numbers but weak rushing and poor pressure management. In a Superflex league that QB archetype can still merit aggressive drafting. In LVE, it usually should not.

## Running backs

Running back evaluation needs the biggest league-specific correction. In full PPR, it is easy to overpay for catches. In this league, pure reception volume is less valuable, but receiving still matters because it predicts snap share, route participation, two-minute role, and first-down opportunities. The more stable RB spine in the public models is draft capital, adjusted production, receiving role, and separate evidence of rushing talent beyond box-score totals. Athletic testing matters less than people think overall, but RB speed score is the most defensible standalone athletic input in the public work gathered here. citeturn27view0turn26view1turn10view0turn31view0

## Wide receivers

Wide receiver is the position where the league format most changes the rookie board. Three WR starters and two flex spots mean WR demand is high every week, and deep benches plus 23 keepers increase your willingness to stash profiles with strong long-term indicators. The research base here aligns well: draft capital matters, but age-adjusted production and target-earning metrics are the center of gravity. Public work repeatedly supports adjusted receiving production, target share or TPRR, and YPRR-like efficiency signals, while also showing that college 1D/RR by itself is not strong enough to function as a standalone core predictor. citeturn24view1turn21view0turn19view1turn22view0turn41view1

## Tight ends

Tight end is where your league context should make the model the most conservative. Deep benches make patience viable, but no TE premium means patience should not be confused with aggression. The better public TE research in this source set says that after adjusting for the position’s dual role, the strongest TE indicators are draft capital plus receiving production and efficiency, with athleticism as a meaningful but still secondary piece. Public academic work also suggests teams have historically leaned too hard on size and combine measures relative to college receiving evidence. citeturn35view1turn14view0turn14view2turn37view0

The tables below are written for deterministic implementation. The intended workflow is:

1. Score each feature on a 0–100 percentile basis within a position-specific historical cohort.
2. Apply the suggested raw weight ranges.
3. Rebase active feature weights to 100 inside each position model.
4. Track **confidence** separately from the base score using `missing_data_penalty`, risk flags, and role clarity.
5. Add a post-draft opportunity layer.
6. Compare every rookie to the released-veteran/free-agent pool before generating the final offline draft board.

Unless noted otherwise, `missing_data_penalty` is best applied to the **confidence score**, not directly to the raw prospect score.

## Open questions and limitations

Some requested micro-metrics have much thinner public validation than others. In particular, position-specific evidence for BMI alone, raw RAS as a standalone feature, and certain conference-label effects is weak or mixed in the public material reviewed here. Where that happened, I treated those variables as guardrails, tie-breakers, or confidence modifiers rather than primary drivers. citeturn17view0turn37view0

Public source coverage is also uneven by metric. The strongest publicly documented signals in this source set are draft capital, age/entry timing, normalized production, target-earning, QB rushing, and TE receiving evidence. The weakest are isolated combine metrics, raw first-down rates by themselves, and narrative film notes without standardization.
