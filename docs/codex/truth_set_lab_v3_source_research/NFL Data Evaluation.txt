Evaluation of Paid vs Free Data for Fantasy/NFL Model Inputs
This evaluation reviews whether the key inputs needed for NFL/fantasy analytics models can be obtained without paid data. Research prioritized official data repositories and community‑maintained datasets (e.g., nflverse/nflfastR) and avoided scraping or sources with Terms‑of‑Service (ToS) risks. Each field was classified into one of five categories:
free_direct – freely available structured data exists.
free_derivable – data can be derived from free sources (e.g., play‑by‑play).
free_proxy_only – only proxy metrics are available for free; exact data require paid data.
paid_likely_required – free sources do not provide the data; paid subscription/licensing is likely needed.
unavailable_or_not_recommended – data are unavailable or retrieval would violate ToS.

Notes on Legal Risks
Low risk means the data is distributed under open‑source/community terms (e.g., nflverse, Pro Football Reference) and can be used for non‑commercial analysis. Users should still review PFR’s terms of use for any commercial applications.
Medium risk applies to manually copying data from official league pages (e.g., NFL fantasy projections). Manual collection for personal analysis is generally acceptable, but automated scraping or redistribution would likely violate terms.
High risk applies where data is proprietary or licensed (Next Gen Stats, FTN, PFF). Attempting to collect these data without permission would infringe ToS and could result in legal issues.
What We Can Do Without Paid Data
Core production metrics – Passing, rushing and receiving yards, touchdowns, and other box‑score stats are fully available through the nflverse/nflreadr datasets. These data include first downs and can be aggregated by game or season[1].
Snap share – Offensive and defensive snap counts and percentages are available from Pro Football Reference via nflreadr[2]. This allows modeling of player usage without paying for premium services.
Derived usage metrics – Using open play‑by‑play data, one can compute target share, RB opportunity share, red‑zone and goal‑line usage by filtering plays by field position and summing rushes/targets. This requires processing but no paid data.
Injury history (through 2024) – nflreadr provides structured injury reports from 2009–2024[5]. Though the dataset is no longer updated, it can still be useful for historical modeling.
These available fields enable building predictive models that account for player production, team usage, snap participation, and historical injuries. With some coding, the derived metrics can approximate real‑world usage patterns without incurring subscription costs.
What Remains Weak Without Paid Data
Routes and route‑based metrics – Variables like routes run, route participation, Targets per Route Run and Yards per Route Run depend on detailed route charting or player tracking data. According to the nflverse team, participation data after 2023 comes from FTN and does not update during the season. There is no open source for routes run, and Next Gen Stats data is proprietary. Consequently, models built solely on free data cannot capture separation, route usage or efficiency per route.
Current injury reports – The injury dataset in nflreadr is no longer updated after 2024 due to the death of its data source. Current‑year injury status must be monitored manually through team injury reports or pay‑walled APIs.
Forward‑looking projections – High‑quality fantasy projections are typically compiled by analysts and available via paid services (PFF, FTN, Fantasy Points, etc.). Free projections exist on NFL.com, but they are not provided in a structured format and may have limited analytics. Without paid projections, one must build their own projection models or rely on unsanctioned scraping.
Safety of Manual ChatGPT‑Agent Collection
Allowed: Downloading and processing data from the nflverse GitHub repositories is safe and does not violate ToS. The agent may use tools to download CSV/RDS files or call load_* functions locally.
Caution: Manually copying projections from NFL.com for a handful of players is likely acceptable for personal use, but automating scraping or redistributing the data could breach terms. Use manual copying only for small‑scale research.
Not allowed: Collecting routes run or participation data from FTN, Next Gen Stats or other subscription services without authorization is unsafe. The agent should not attempt to bypass paywalls or scrape these sites.
Recommended Implementation Order
Integrate free nflverse datasets. Start by ingesting player stats (load_player_stats) and snap counts (load_snap_counts) into your modeling pipeline. Compute production metrics, first downs and snap share.
Derive usage metrics. Use play‑by‑play data from nflfastR to calculate target share, RB opportunity share, red‑zone and goal‑line usage. This step requires coding but no additional data purchases.
Incorporate historical injury reports. Pull injury data through 2024 from load_injuries[5]. For 2025 onward, plan to ingest public team injury reports manually or from news feeds until a new open source emerges.
Build internal projection model. Because high‑quality projections are not freely available, develop your own projection system using historical player performance, usage metrics, team tendencies and schedule. Alternatively, use limited free projections from NFL.com as a baseline.
Evaluate need for paid data. Once the free‑data models are operational, assess whether adding route‑based metrics (routes run, route participation, TPRR, YPRR) would materially improve predictive accuracy. If so, consider licensing data from providers like FTN or PFF. Ensure that any paid data usage complies with licensing agreements.
By following this order, you can build a robust analytics foundation using open data and only later decide whether investing in paid data is worthwhile.
